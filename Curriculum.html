ENIS Robots 3.0 — Upgraded, Homologated & Back in the Arena

Engineering Geeks Club (EGC) — National Robotics Competition, Sfax

Competition

Two months after ENICarthage, we were back — but not with the same robot. Every competition teaches you something, and what ENICarthage taught us was clear: the robot needed to do more than move. ENIS Robots 3.0 had strict homologation requirements, and to qualify, our robot had to be capable of picking up an object from a 15 cm elevation and delivering it precisely to unlock a gate — then continue navigating an obstacle course to the finish.

So we engineered an upgrade: a servo-actuated elevator gripper arm mounted on the front of the chassis — controllable in real time, precise enough to grab a target object and release it on command. The robot also got a structural rebuild: reinforced aluminum chassis panels, blue LED headlights for visibility, and cleaner internal wiring to survive the abuse of competition terrain.

The challenge itself was a timed run — not just obstacle avoidance, but a full sequence: approach the elevated object, grip it, lower it, deliver it to the gate trigger, watch the gate open, then race through the remaining course before time runs out. Every second counted. Every mechanism had to work on the first try. That's the kind of pressure that turns builders into engineers.

Arduino Servo Control Mechanical Design Gripper / Elevator Mechanism DC Motor Control Bluetooth Control Chassis Fabrication Electronics Wiring Timed Challenge Strategy
  • Engineered a servo-actuated elevator gripper arm that could pick up an object from a 15 cm elevated platform and deliver it to a target zone — a required homologation feature for competition entry.
  • Rebuilt the robot chassis with reinforced aluminum panels and improved internal layout, addressing structural failures observed during the previous competition.
  • Integrated blue LED headlights and cleaner cable routing — improving both aesthetics and reliability under competition stress.
  • Executed a full timed sequence under competition pressure: object pickup → delivery → gate trigger → obstacle navigation → finish line, with all mechanisms required to function on the first attempt.
  • Learned the critical difference between a robot that works on a bench and one that performs reliably in a live, timed, high-stakes environment.

ENICarthage Robots 5.0 — First Steps in Competitive Robotics

Engineering Geeks Club (EGC) — National Robotics Competition, Tunis

Competition

Every engineer remembers their first competition. Mine was ENICarthage Robots 5.0, organized by the Engineering Geeks Club at ENIC Carthage — one of Tunisia's most anticipated student robotics events. Dozens of teams, a packed outdoor arena, and hundreds of eyes watching every move your robot makes.

Our entry was a Bluetooth-controlled ground robot, built from scratch: a custom chassis, DC motors with rubber-grip wheels, a motor driver, and an Arduino brain — all commanded in real time from a smartphone app. The arena itself was a challenge course designed to test navigation, precision, and control under pressure — ramps, gravel zones, obstacles, and tight corridors that punished any sloppy driving.

The robot took hits. The chassis cracked. The wiring held. And standing there in front of that crowd, phone in hand, watching something I built with my own hands move through that course — that was the moment I understood why engineers build things. Not just to make them work, but to make them perform.

Arduino Bluetooth Control Embedded Systems DC Motor Control Motor Driver (L298N) Chassis Design Android App Control Real-time Control Prototyping
  • Designed and assembled a ground robot from scratch — chassis fabrication, motor mounting, electronics integration, and wiring — with zero budget for mistakes on competition day.
  • Implemented Bluetooth serial communication between an Arduino microcontroller and an Android smartphone, enabling real-time directional control of the robot.
  • Configured a DC motor driver (L298N) for bidirectional wheel control — forward, reverse, left and right turns — with responsive speed handling.
  • Navigated a multi-terrain competition course including ramps, gravel, and obstacle zones under time pressure and in front of a live audience.
  • First hands-on experience with competitive engineering: designing for robustness, debugging under pressure, and performing in front of a crowd — skills no classroom teaches.

First Contact with Industrial Automation

ISET Sidi Bouzid — Festo MPS Pneumatic & PLC Training Lab

University Lab

Before any simulation software, before TIA Portal on a laptop — there was this: a real Festo Modular Production System bolted to a lab bench, hissing with compressed air and alive with sensor signals. This is where my hands first touched industrial automation.

The task was to program and control a Pick & Place station — built from pneumatic cylinders, solenoid valves, pressure regulators, and proximity sensors, all coordinated through a PLC running Siemens STEP 7 and programmed in Grafcet (SFC). The station had to pick a workpiece, transfer it via a gantry-style XY axis, and place it precisely at a target position — entirely autonomously.

It sounds straightforward — until you're staring at 40+ pneumatic fittings and a control panel that demands you understand every I/O address before the first cylinder extends. That pressure — the real kind — taught me what no simulation ever could: how to read a system before you dare command it.

Siemens STEP 7 Grafcet / SFC Ladder Logic Pneumatic Actuators Solenoid Valves Proximity Sensors Festo MPS I/O Wiring Pressure Regulation
  • Programmed a full Pick & Place sequence using Grafcet (SFC) — coordinating pneumatic cylinder extensions, gripper actuation, and sensor feedback in a safe sequential flow.
  • Wired and validated all digital I/O connections between the PLC and Festo MPS station, mapping physical sensor/actuator addresses to STEP 7 symbol table entries.
  • Understood pneumatic circuit logic: air supply management, pressure regulation, directional control valves, and double-acting cylinder behavior under load.
  • Debugged real timing conflicts between actuator strokes and sensor feedback — a critical skill that simulation software never exposes.
  • First hands-on exposure to industrial control philosophy: fail-safe design, emergency stop logic, and controlled state transitions.
